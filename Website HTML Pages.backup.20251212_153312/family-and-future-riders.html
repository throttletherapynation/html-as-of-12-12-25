The TTN™ Family Support Framework.

Our goal is to help families go from “We’d love to, but…” to “We’re so glad we did.” Everything we build runs through a simple lens: Ride. Heal. Belong.

Ride • Skills & Seat Time

Making the First Rides Feel Safe — Not Scary.

We focus on real-world conditions, not just highlight reels. That means selecting parks and routes where new riders and kids can learn without being thrown into terrain that breaks machines or rattles confidence.

  • Guidance on picking parks that match your crew’s skill range.
  • Seat-time building approach — short, supported loops before big days.
  • Education that respects fear, fatigue and sensory overload.
Heal • Mental & Emotional Space

Using the Ride to Breathe, Not Escape.

For many families, off-road time is less about adrenaline and more about exhale. We honor the fact that some riders are healing from grief, burnout, medical challenges or life changes.

  • Language and spaces that make it okay to ride at your own pace.
  • Support for riders processing big life chapters, not just big climbs.
  • Encouragement to use the trails as a place to reconnect, not numb out.

The Family Ride Readiness Checklist.

You don’t have to be perfect to take your family riding. You just need a clear starting point. Use this checklist as a living tool — something you revisit before each season or big trip.

  • Everyone knows the plan. Where you’re riding, how long you’ll be out, what the backup plan is if someone is tired, overstimulated or done for the day.
  • Gear fits the people actually wearing it. Helmets, harnesses, eye protection, footwear and layers checked for kids who grew and adults whose needs changed.
  • Machines are pre-checked. Fluids, tires, lug nuts, belts, lights, radios and anything that keeps you rolling safely — especially when kids are on board.
  • Roles are clear. Who leads? Who sweeps? Who rides in which rig? Who is the “call it” person if conditions change or someone needs to turn back?
  • “Tap out” is respected. Everyone understands that saying “I’m done” is not failure — it’s part of keeping the whole crew safe and supported.
  • Weather and daylight are on your side. Plan around nap windows, heat, cold and how far you are from camp or town when the sun starts to drop.
  • Communication works. Radios or cell coverage tested where possible, and simple hand signals agreed on before you leave camp.
